Realization of Metallic Boron in Monolayer Structures.

By Baojie Feng, Jin Zhang, Ro-Ya Liu, Iimori Takushi, Chao Lian, Lan Chen, Kehui Wu, Hui Li, Sheng Meng, Fumio Komori, Iwao Matsuda

Boron, the fifth element in the periodic table, is a fascinating material with rich chemical properties. The bonding states in boron are rather complex, ranging from two-center bond to multi-center bond, forming a large family of allotropes. Although boron has been discovered for more than two centuries, its crystalline form was only synthesized half a century ago. Until now, only several allotropes of crystalline boron have been confirmed to be stable: {\alpha}-rhombohedral, \b{eta}-rhombohedral, T-192 and {\gamma}-B28, and most of these allotropes are semiconductors, with a large band gap at the Fermi level. Since the discovery of graphene in 2004, there is a boom of research on monolayer materials, such as silicene, germanene and so on. Monolayer boron has also been intensively studied using first-principles calculations. However, experimental synthesis of monolayer boron has been realized only until recently. In contrast to the semiconducting properties of its bulk allotropes, monolayer boron is predicted to be metallic. The metallic boron is complementary to its semiconductor allotropes, making it possible to realize the high-efficient all-boron nano-devices. However, the electronic properties of boron remain to be understood. Here, we synthesized monolayer boron on Ag(111) and determined its band structure using angle resolved photoelectron spectroscopy (ARPES). We observed one electron pocket at the S point and one pair of electron pockets near the X point, which agrees well with our first-principles calculation results. The chemical properties have been investigated by in-situ X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS). Our results provide the first experimental evidence for the metallic character of monolayer boron.
